So what is Property-Based Testing?  You might think it’s the old-fashioned idea that one must be . . .

. . . a huge number of tests that are identical except that they test different examples, what's usually called example-based testing.  Maybe they're expecting different outputs, but maybe not.  Instead, in property-based testing, we write . . . 

def test_domain_1_aspect_1
  ...
end

def test_domain_1_aspect_2
  ...
end

def test_domain_2_aspect_1
  ...
end

def test_domain_2_aspect_2
  ...
end
. . . a small number of tests, and describe the type of examples each one applies to, and some desired property of the output, perhaps in relation to the input.  It may take multiple such tests to cover the whole domain of valid inputs if there are multiple kinds, or to test all the desired relationships and properties, but almost always many fewer tests than in an example-based approach.  In fact, sometimes we may be able to write just one test.  The PBT tool, which may be a library or a framework, so from here on I'm just going to say tool, will . . .
Image: https://pxhere.com/en/photo/615255
. . . randomly generate lots of inputs, and examine the outputs.  Those of you who have done much work in the security space might be thinking it sounds somewhat like . . .
Image: https://www.wannapik.com/vectors/15576
. . . “fuzzing”, and yes it’s very similar.  In fact, the core of one of the major PBT tools, Hypothesis, is a fuzzing library, Conjecture.  The difference is mainly in the intent.  Fuzzing is mainly for finding the limits of validity, and seeing what happens when we exceed them, especially inducing specific unintended behaviors without making the system crash.  However, property-based testing is for when we know the limits of validity (or at least we think we do), and we want to verify that the output is correct for all valid inputs -- or at least as many of them as we can reasonably sample. That . . .
Image: https://vectorportal.com/vector/price-reduction-sticker.ai/21963
. . . reduction in the number of tests is the main advantage of Property-Based Testing, over the standard approach of writing a bunch of examples. It also reduces implicit human . . .
Image: PD, from https://www.thefemalelead.com/post/navigating-gender-bias-in-the-workplace-lessons-from-tackling-racial-bias
. . . bias, like how some thing can pass tests with human-scale input numbers, but might blow up or or have errors with something huge.

For instance, suppose some step in our algorithm involves multiplying two input numbers, that could have any 64-bit value, with the result put into another 64-bit number.  It should work fine with numbers that humans are likely to use, up to maybe hundreds or thousands.  However, it’s quite easy to create a bug in that sort of situation, mainly integer overflow, or underflow, such as by multiplying two numbers that are each at least a few billion.  If they're both beyond the range of 32-bit numbers, their product will be beyond the range of 64 bit numbers.

By sampling the rest of the range, Property-Based Testing can help point out how the term "valid input" . . .
Image: all over the web!
. . . doesn't mean what we think it means.  This will help us think about our valid input ranges.  Then we can restrict the inputs by other means, such as catching invalid values programmatically, or declaring a more restrictive type, if our language of choice has such a feature.

To clarify the idea, let’s try an example.  Let’s suppose we’re writing tests for everybody's favorite interview whiteboarding classic . . . 
Is x a . . .  multiple of 3?
           _____Y_________N___
multiple  Y| FizzBuzz | Buzz |
of 5?     N| Fizz     | x    |
. . . FizzBuzz.  Does anybody need an explanation of FizzBuzz?

(IF ANYBODY:) I'm not sure how common this is over here, but at least in the USA, this is a children's game, used in schools, to drill the idea of multiples.  It starts at 1, usually up to 100, or until only one child remains in the game.  Each child in turn says the right thing, as outlined in this table.  If the number is a multiple of three but not five, they say Fizz instead of the number.  If it's a multiple of five but not three, they say Buzz.  If it's a multiple of both, they say FizzBuzz.  If it's not a multiple of either, they say the number.  So it should start 1, 2, Fizz, 4, Buzz, Fizz, 7, and so on. If they say the wrong thing, they're out of the game.  Is that clear enough?

This is often used as a basic filter in hiring developers.  If we can't quickly whip up an implementation of FizzBuzz, the thinking goes that we probably don't really now how to program very well -- or maybe we perform very poorly under pressure, or of course maybe we're not at all familiar with FizzBuzz.

So let's look at how we would usually test an implementation of this, by writing example-based tests, each looking for the correct response for a given input number.  The typical path would be to test 3, 5, 15, and some number that’s not a multiple of 3 nor 5, let's . . .
Image: https://xkcd.com/221/
. . . randomly pick 4, and call it a day.  But I’m sure we can all think of easy implementations that would give the correct answer for those numbers, but not necessarily for any others, like . . . 

case number
  when  3 then "Fizz"
  when  4 then "4"
  when  5 then "Buzz"
  when 15 then "FizzBuzz"
  else         "Garbage!"
end
. . . so.  For our carefully chosen examples, sure it returns the correct response . . . but for anything else, literally garbage.  Or maybe we’d get a bit more complex, and also test another set, like 6, 10, 30, and 8.  

case number
  when  3,  6 then "Fizz"
  when  4,  8 then "4"
  when  5, 10 then "Buzz"
  when 15, 30 then "FizzBuzz"
  else             "Garbage!"
end
The same idea applies, no matter what small set of numbers we pick!  This sort of approach may look familiar to those of you familiar with

Images: https://freesvg.org/ultra-smart-owl, https://freesvg.org/cartoon-smiling-donkey,
https://openclipart.org/detail/320774/boy-and-girl-play-ping-pong, https://www.pexels.com/photo/two-men-looking-at-a-laptop-4974920/
Smart-Ass Ping-Pong Pair Programming.  What's that, you might be wondering?  I'll assume, despite what happens then, that you're familiar with the concepts of pair programming and test-driven development.  (If you're not, Google them after this presentation.)  One way of combining them is ping-pong pair progamming.  In this, one partner writes a test, and the other must write, in accordance with Test-Driven Development principles, the simplest possible code to make that test pass, without breaking any existing tests.  The second partner then writes a test, for the first one to make pass, with the simplest possible code.  And so on, back and forth, until all aspects of the current task are implemented.  One particular variety of ping-pong pair programming, one not usually practiced in industry but rather as fun practice, is Smart-Ass Ping Pong Pair Programming, in which each partner tries to avoid actually implementing the feature correctly, making the other person do that work.  The tricky part is to write a test such that actually implementing the feature correctly, is easier than adding a special case for some new example we choose.  Code like we saw on the previous slide (SHOW IT!) could certainly be generated by smart-ass ping-pong pair programming, one example at a time.  Implementing it correctly may be easier than writing all those cases, but it's harder than just adding one more.

Anyway, back to Property-Based Testing, what we really want to do is test that
Ax ∈ valid range, f(x) is correct
it gives correct answers for all valid inputs.  (Or at least, as many as we want to let it test, within constraints such as time, CPU cycles, etc.)  It would be great if we could divide that input space into easily described sets.  Well, we can, just as I did five slides ago!  The usual valid range of inputs is integers from 1 to 100, but even expanding it to all integers, even negative ones, all of them are either multiples of three but not five, the other way around, both, or neither.  We can write just one test for each of these four cases, let our PBT tool create random inputs, let it run for some reasonable amount of time or iterations or whatever, and have very strong confidence that we've tested a significant sampling of the input space, enough to have strong confidence in the correctness of our code.  That's already only half the number of tests that we would have written for this implementation, without putting much of a serious dent in the input space. So how do we write a test for such an entire case, not just one number, especially without violating the testing principle of "one assertion per test"?  With most property-based testing tools , it's very similar to . . . 

def test_6_is_fizz
  expected = "Fizz"
  actual = fizzbuzz(6)
  assert(actual == expected)
end
. . . writing one example-based test, except that . . . 

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
  expected = "Fizz"
  actual = fizzbuzz(number)
  assert(actual == expected)
end
. . . we usually call a generator to get the number (and we'll talk soon about those).  The other main difference is that there is usually some way to tell the tool how long to keep running the test, such as a number of milliseconds, or iterations, or whatever.

So what's . . .
Image: https://openclipart.org/detail/335427/portable-generator
. . . a generator?  A generator is a function to randomly generate valid test data.  They can be as simple as calling a random-number function each time, or they can get more complex, doing things like keeping track of what's already been covered, so that the next call can go into uncharted territory.  They can be primitives like in this case, slightly more complex things like lists, or even custom things, such as objects (or records or maps or whatever our language calls them).  Usually this is where we would incorporate constraints, such as "give me an integer, not just any integer, but one from 1 to 100, inclusive, that is a multiple of three, but not of five".  That might look like this: 

MAX_VALUE = 100

def generate_mult_of_3_not_5
  max_rand = MAX_VALUE/3  # integer div
  candidate = random(max_rand+1) * 3
  if candidate % 5 == 0
    return generate_mult_of_3_not_5
  else
    return candidate
  end
end
There are lots of ways we could implement this, like using a loop instead of recursing until we get a valid answer, and of course I'm omitting keeping track of previous results just to keep it simple.  The quality of our generator can drastically affect the ease of writing our property-based tests, and their effectiveness.  For instance, if we're testing a name capitalization function, using a random string generator won't be as good as a generator aimed at realistic names.  Our tool may provide such a thing, or something usable that way with certain arguments, but we may have to write one ourselves.

Most of the tools supply a few kinds of generators, and some of them have very flexible ones, for which the call might look more like this: 

generate_integer(min: 1,
                 max: 100,
                 multiple_of: 3,
                 not_multiple_of: 5)
We could either use a call like that every time, or wrap it in helper functions with names like the ones I used before, or whatever else we might want to do to make it a bit easier to read or write.

Anyway, we can tell our PBT tool, via each test, the definition of our case, and the proper response.  In the first three cases, they’re just constant strings, so their property-based tests are very simple: 

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == "Fizz")
end
This is a shortened version of the test that I showed you about four slides back, for numbers that are multiples of three but not five. 

def test_mult_of_5_not_3_is_buzz
  assert(fizzbuzz(generate_mult_of_5_not_3())
    == "Buzz")
end
This would be an even shorter version of the opposite case, multiples of five but not three.  And as long as I'm exploring slightly different styles, if we're using a more pipeline-oriented language, such as Elixir, that's a handy way to clean up that nested mess, so it might look something like this: 

def test_mult_of_5_not_3_is_buzz do
  generate_mult_of_5_not_3()
  |> fizzbuzz()
  |> assert_equals("Buzz")
end
But since most languages aren't like that, we'll get back to fairly normal syntax, with which . . . 

def test_mult_of_15_is_fizzbuzz
  number = generate_mult_of_15()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == "Fizzbuzz")
end
. . . this would be the test for multiples of fifteen.

In the fourth case though, 

def test_mult_of_neither_is_number
  number = generate_non_mult_of_3_and_5()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == number.to_string)
end
we have to transform the input from a number to a string.  Still not at all a big deal.

So here we see that using property-based tests, we can write just four tests, let it run for a while, and have fairly thorough sampling of the input space, as opposed to spending human time writing lots of examples.  It's a great example of the adage that we should let the computer do the boring grunt work.  In this case that boring grunt work is just the selection of examples, once we've done the brainwork of describing the categories of examples, and the expected response for each.

Now, this is a very simple set of tests, so much so that one might complain that we’re effectively mimicking the implementation, and that’s a fair accusation.  So now let’s look at something a bit more complex, one of the canonical examples in introductions to property based testing: reversing a list.  We don’t care about how it’s implemented, but we can take advantage of the relationship between the input and output: if we reverse the output, we get the input again.  So what we can do is: 

def generate_random_list(max_len, max_val)
  len = random(max_len)
  return [1..len].map { random(max_val) }
end
first write a generator to make up a random length, and return a list of that many random numbers. 

def test_reverse_reverses
  original = generate_random_list(10, 10)
  reversed = my_reverse(original)
  reversed_twice = my_reverse(reversed)
  assert(reversed_twice == original)
end
In our test we can call the generator, hold onto what it gives us, run it through our reversal algorithm, and test that if we run the result through the reversal algorithm again, we get the original list.

However, there are many other things one could do that would have the same effect, like negating each number, without affecting the order of the list.  (And in fact we can use this same idea, of running it through twice, to test a negation function.)  But we can make it at least a little bit harder to fake, by also 

def test_reverse_swaps_first_and_last
  original = generate_random_list(10, 10)
  reversed = my_reverse(original)
  assert(reversed.first == original.last)
  assert(reversed.last == original.first)
end
testing that the first element of the original is the last element of the result, and vice-versa.  Yes, this may occasionally pass with an incorrect algorithm, but that's why we test several different aspects of the functionality, and many repetitions.

Sometimes it can be hard to find and encapsulate good properties, but there are some useful guidelines.  Scott Wlaschin made
Scott Wlaschin's Seven Kinds of Properties:
  • Different paths, same destination
  • There and back again
  • Some things never change
  • The more things change,
    the more they stay the same
  • Solve a smaller problem first
  • Hard to prove, easy to verify
  • The test oracle
a list of seven approaches.  The graphics on the following slides are shamelessly stolen from his post titled "Choosing properties for property-based testing" on his blog at FSharpForFunAndProfit.com.
Different paths, same destination

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"Different paths, same destination" is about doing operations in a different order, but getting the same result.  It's very useful when it's the same or related operations, like addition and subtraction, or division and multiplication, so that the order doesn't actually matter, or in math terms it's commutative.  For instance, multiplying by 10 and then dividing by 3 gets us the same result as dividing by 3 and then multiplying by 10... at least ignoring floating-point problems.
There and back again

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"There and back again" is about combining an operation with the inverse operation, so that we get the original value.  For instance, add and subtract, multiply and divide, and even setting and then verifying some value, like writing a value to disk and reading it back, using a setter and a getter on a property of an object, or serialization and deserialization.  Sometimes an operation is its own inverse, such as reversing a list or negating a number, as mentioned earlier.
Some things never change

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"Some things never change" is about invariants that are perserved through a transformation.  For instance, when we sort a list, its size and members are preserved, only their order changes.
The more things change,
the more they stay the same

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"The more things change, the more they stay the same" is about idempotence, the idea that an operation can be applied multiple times and the result will always be the same as the first time.  The example he gives is selecting the distinct members of a list, but it is a useful principle in programming, to make sure that a loop run amok doesn't do too much damage.  It's particularly important in message and event processing, in which an item may be processed more than once.
Solve a smaller problem first

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"Solve a smaller problem first" is about the logical principle of induction -- if we can prove some idea about a smaller piece, and prove that adding onto it doesn't break the idea, we can prove it about the whole thing, by induction.  This is very useful for recursive structures like lists and trees.
Hard to prove, easy to verify

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"Hard to prove, easy to verify" is I think a bit misnamed, and should really be "Hard to solve, easy to verify".  In this formulation, it should sound familiar to the theory geeks, as this is this basis of the whole debate over whether P = NP, and no I'm not going to delve into that further.  The idea behind this is that the function we're testing may be very hairy and take a long time, but it may be very quick to verify that it worked, such as in this example verifying that the route it found does indeed get from the start to the end of the maze.  The goal is that the verification is fast enough to use in a test, ideally one executed many many times, as is very common in property-based testing.
The test oracle

Image: from https://fsharpforfunandprofit.com/posts/property-based-testing-2/
"The test oracle" is about comparing two ways of doing something.  This is particularly useful when we're changing from one algorithm, that we know works, to another, that we are implementing.  This could be a small bit of internals, or a whole feature, like in the Strangler Fig pattern, or indeed the entire system.  We get a result from the one we know works, and see if the new one comes up with the same answer.

Now, do we really need a property-based testing
Image: https://easy-peasy.ai/ai-image-generator/images/electrician-toolkit-20-essential-tools-hd
tool to do this?  It makes the setup easier, but any decently experienced programmer should be able to write that from scratch, maybe even make their own framework or library for it, so it becomes a tradeoff, based mostly on how much of it we're doing.  For instance, 

PASSES = 100

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  for pass_num in 1..PASSES do
    number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
    expected = "Fizz"
    actual = fizzbuzz(number)
    assert(actual == expected)
  end
end
here is one of those FizzBuzz tests, in plain old MiniTest, the test runner that ships with Ruby, set to run some number of times.

And 

SECONDS = 0.1

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  end_time = Time.now + SECONDS
  while Time.now < end_time do
    number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
    expected = "Fizz"
    actual = fizzbuzz(number)
    assert(actual == expected)
  end
end
here it is set to run for some number of seconds, in this case a fraction of a second.  And we could certainly use both constraints at once.  While it's clearly possible to do all this without a PBT tool, at least using one would make it easier to write, and probably clearer for whoever has to maintain our test suite.  (Keep in mind, that may well be "future us"!)

However, the real magic of a property-based testing tool is in something much more difficult, called “shrinking”.  Not quite all tools do this, but the idea is to find the edges, by searching between an input that passes the test, and one that doesn’t, when it finds one of each.

Fizzbuzz has a repeating cycle of 15, but in many other cases, the rules or results change across the domain.  Let’s look at 

@n: Integer
def stupid_function(Integer n)
  1 / ((n / 5.0).round - 10)
end
a simple function that divides 1 by an input integer, but first it takes that input, divides it by five, rounds to the nearest integer, and subtracts ten.  Our output should be a floating point number in the range -1 to 1, but in some range of inputs, assuming we haven’t taken steps to avoid it, we’re going to have a division by zero error.  That would be the range where dividing by five and rounding gets us ten, or in other words, without rounding we get 9.6 to 10.4, or in other other words, 
47 -> -1
48 -> /0 ERROR
49 -> /0 ERROR
50 -> /0 ERROR
51 -> /0 ERROR
52 -> /0 ERROR
53 ->  1
48 to 52.

Suppose we've told our tool the input range is integers from 1 to 100, and it successfully does 1, and 100, and then tries 50, but of course blows up.  First of course it tells us that, and now we know our truly valid input range isn't quite what we thought it was.

But wait!  There's more!

It figures, if I may anthropomorphize it a bit, that there is probably an edge case, possibly multiple, somewhere between 50 and each of those numbers.  Let's look at it trying to find the edge case between 1 and 50.  It will basically do a binary search, trying halfway at 
26 -> -1
38 -> -1
44 -> -1
47 -> -1
49 -> /0 ERROR
48 -> /0 ERROR
26, the middle of the remainder (so basically 3/4 of the way) at 38, 7/8 of the way at 44, 15/16 of the way at 47, and finally gets another error, 31/32 of the way up, at 49.  There's only one number left to investigate between success and failure, at 48, which gives it another error, so now it can report to us that it found an edge case between 47 and 48.  Most of the tools will also investigate the other side, using a similar technique, and tell us about the edge between 52 and 53.  Not only will revealing such failures in testing, rather than production, save us from outages later, but also, having a tool apply this idea of shrinking saves immense amounts of time we might otherwise spend tracking down exactly where those edge cases lie.

With a more complex input, or set of inputs, a PBT tool will try to simplify it.  For instance, if it detected a failure in our sorting algorithm, it would try to shrink the input to the shortest list that makes it fail.  With an object, it might try to leave as many properties as possible empty, or filled in with default values.

The first usage of the term is generally thought to be the 1994 paper titled "Towards a Property-based Testing Environment with Applications to Security-Critical Software" by George Fink, Calvin Ko, Myla Archer, and Karl Levitt.  The idea got a major boost in 1999, when Koen Claessen and John Hughes developed a tool called QuickCheck, for the Haskell programming language.  Not only was this the first major property-based testing tool, but it was also the origin of the idea of shrinking.

Even so, it didn't penetrate the mainstream much until about 2015, when David MacIver released Hypothesis, a PBT library for Python, certainly a much more popular language than Haskell!  Since then, there has been an explosion of PBT tools for most major programming languages, and we'll get to a list later.

Now that you know about the powerful technique of Property-Based Testing, and probably at least one tool to make it much easier, I encourage you to go try it on your projects -- once you get used to thinking about generalities rather than trying to come up with specific examples, you'll find you can make your test suite far more exhaustive, while actually writing fewer tests yourself.
