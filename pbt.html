So what is Property-Based Testing?  You might think it’s the old-fashioned idea that you have to be . . .

. . . a landowner in order to vote, but no, that’s not it. Property-Based Testing, or PBT, is a technique that lets you stop writing . . .

  ...

. . . a huge number of tests that are identical except that they test different examples, perhaps expecting different outputs, but maybe not.  Instead, in PBT, you write . . . 

def test_domain_1_aspect_1
  ...
end

def test_domain_1_aspect_2
  ...
end

def test_domain_2_aspect_1
  ...
end

def test_domain_2_aspect_2
  ...
end
. . . a small number of tests, and describe the type of examples each one applies to, and some desired property of the output, perhaps in relation to the input.  It may take multiple such tests to cover the whole domain of valid inputs if there are multiple kinds, or to test all the desired relationships and properties, but sometimes you may be able to write just one test.  The PBT framework will . . .
Image: https://pxhere.com/en/photo/615255
. . . randomly generate lots of inputs, and examine the outputs.  Those of you who have done much work in the security space might be thinking it sounds like . . .
Image: https://www.wannapik.com/vectors/15576
. . . “fuzzing”, and yes it’s very similar.  The difference is mainly in the intent.  Fuzzing is mainly for finding the limits of validity, and seeing what happens when you exceed them, while PBT is for when you know the limits of validity (or at least you think you do), and want to verify that the output is correct for all valid inputs -- or at least as many of them as you can reasonably sample. That . . .
Image: https://vectorportal.com/vector/price-reduction-sticker.ai/21963
. . . reduction in the number of tests is the main advantage it has, over the standard approach of writing a bunch of examples. It also reduces implicit human . . .
Image: PD, from https://www.thefemalelead.com/post/navigating-gender-bias-in-the-workplace-lessons-from-tackling-racial-bias
. . . bias, like how some thing can pass tests with human-scale input numbers, but might blow up or or have errors with something huge. By sampling the rest of the range, PBT can help point out how the term "valid input" . . .
Image: all over the web!
. . . doesn't mean what you think it means.  This will help you think about your types, especially declaring limitations on the valid ranges.

Now let’s try an example.  Let’s suppose you’re writing tests for everybody's favorite interview whiteboarding classic . . . 
Is x a . . .   multiple of 3?
             ____Y_________N__
multiple  Y | FizzBuzz | Buzz |
of 5?     N | Fizz     | x    |
. . . FizzBuzz.  Does anybody need an explanation of FizzBuzz?

(IF ANYBODY:) I'm not sure how common this is over here, but at least in the USA, this is a children's game, used in schools, to drill the idea of multiples.  It starts at 1, usually up to 100, or until only one child remains.  Each child in turn says the right thing, as outlined in this table.  If the number is a multiple of three but not five, they say Fizz instead of the number.  If it's a multiple of five but not three, they say Buzz.  If it's a multiple of both, they say FizzBuzz.  If it's not a multiple of either, they say the number.  So it should start 1, 2, Fizz, 4, Buzz, Fizz, 7, and so on. If they say the wrong thing, they're out.  Is that clear enough?

So let's look at how we would usually test an implementation of this, giving the correct response for a given number input.  The typical path would be to test 3, 5, 15, and some number that’s not a multiple of 3 nor 5, let's . . .
Image: https://xkcd.com/221/
. . . randomly pick 4, and call it a day.  But I’m sure we can all think of easy implementations that would give the correct answer for those numbers, but not necessarily for any others, like . . . 

case number
  when  3 then "Fizz"
  when  4 then "4"
  when  5 then "Buzz"
  when 15 then "FizzBuzz"
  else         "Garbage!"
end
. . . so.  Or maybe we’d get a bit more complex, and test 6, 10, 30, and 8.  

case number
  when  3,  6 then "Fizz"
  when  4,  8 then "4"
  when  5, 10 then "Buzz"
  when 15, 30 then "FizzBuzz"
  else             "Garbage!"
end
The same applies, no matter what small set of numbers we pick. What we really want to do is test that it gives correct answers for all valid input numbers.  (Or at least as many as we want to let it test within constraints such as time, CPU cycles, etc.)  It would be great if we could divide that input space into easily described sets.  Well, we can, just as I did a few slides ago!  The usual valid range of inputs is integers from 1 to 100, but even expanding it to all integers, all of them are either multiples of three but not five, the other way around, both, or neither.  We can write just one test for each of these four cases, let our PBT framework create random inputs, let it run for some reasonable amount of time or iterations or whatever, and have very strong confidence that we've tested a significant sampling of the input space.  That's already only half the tests we had written for this, without putting much of a serious dent in the input space. So how do we write a test for such an entire case, not just one number, especially without violating the testing principle of "one assertion per test"?  With most frameworks, it's very similar to . . . 

def test_6_is_fizz
  expected = 'Fizz'
  actual = fizzbuzz(6)
  assert(actual == expected)
end
. . . writing one example-based test, except that . . . 

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
  expected = 'Fizz'
  actual = fizzbuzz(number)
  assert(actual == expected)
end
. . . you usually call a generator to get the number (and we'll talk soon about those).  The other main difference is that there is usually some way to tell the framework how long to keep running the test, such as a number of milliseconds, or iterations, or whatever. So what's . . .
Image: https://openclipart.org/detail/335427/portable-generator
. . . a generator?  A generator is a function to randomly generate valid test data.  They can be primitives like in this case, slightly more complex things like lists, or even custom things, such as objects (or records or maps or whatever your language calls them).  Usually this is where you would incorporate constraints, such as "give me an integer, not just any integer, but one from 1 to 100, inclusive, that is a multiple of three, but not of five".  That might look like this: 

MAX_VALUE = 100

def generate_mult_of_3_not_5
  candidate = (random(MAX_VALUE/3) + 1) * 3
  if candidate % 5 == 0
    return generate_mult_of_3_not_5
  else
    return candidate
  end
end
We could complicate the code by making a multiple of fifteen, then adding some number between 1 and 14 that's not 5 or 10, but this is simpler, I trust you get the idea. We can tell our PBT tool what is the definition and the proper response, for each of these.  In the first three cases, they’re just constant strings, so their property-based tests are very simple: 

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == 'Fizz')
end
This is a shortened version of the test that I showed you a few slides back, for numbers that are multiples of three but not five. 

def test_mult_of_5_not_3_is_buzz
  number = generate_mult_of_5_not_3()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == 'Buzz')
end
This would be the opposite case, multiples of five but not three. 

def test_mult_of_15_is_fizzbuzz
  number = generate_mult_of_15()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == 'Fizzbuzz')
end
And this would be for multiples of fifteen. 

def test_mult_of_neither_is_number
  number = generate_non_mult_of_3_and_5()
  assert(fizzbuzz(number) == number.to_string)
end
In the fourth case though, we have to transform the input from a number to a string.  Not at all a big deal.

So here we see that using property-based tests, we can write just four tests, and have very strong sampling of the input space, as opposed to writing lots of examples.

Now this is a very simple set of tests, so much so that you might complain that we’re effectively mimicking the implementation, and that’s a fair accusation.  So now let’s look at something a bit more complex, one of the canonical examples in PBT: reversing a list.  We don’t care about how it’s implemented, but we can take advantage of the relationship between the input and output: if you reverse the output, you get the input again.  So what we can do is: 

def generate_random_list(max_len, max_val)
  len = random(max_len)
  return [1..len].map { random(max_val) }
end
first write a generator to make up a random length, and return a list of that many random numbers. 

def test_reverse_reverses
  original = generate_random_list(10, 10)
  reversed = my_reverse(original)
  reversed_twice = my_reverse(reversed)
  assert reversed_twice == original
end
In our test we can call the generator, hold onto what it gives us, run it through our reversal algorithm, and test that if you run the result through the reversal algorithm again, you get the original list.

However, there are many other things one could do that would have the same effect, like negating each number.  (And in fact we can use this same technique to test a negation function.)  But we can make it at least a little bit harder to fake, by also 

def test_reverse_swaps_first_and_last
  original = generate_random_list(10, 10)
  reversed = my_reverse(original)
  assert reversed.first == original.last
  assert reversed.last == original.first
end
testing that the first element of the original is the last element of the result, and vice-versa.  Yes, this may occasionally pass with an incorrect algorithm, but that's why we test several different aspects of the functionality, and many repetitions.

Now, do you really need a PBT tool to do this?  For some things, it makes the setup easier, but any decently experienced programmer should be able to write that from scratch, maybe even make their own framework for it.  For instance, 

PASSES = 100

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  for pass_num in 1..PASSES do
    number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
    expected = 'Fizz'
    actual = fizzbuzz(number)
    assert(actual == expected)
  end
end
here is one of those FizzBuzz tests, in plain old MiniTest, the test runner that ships with Ruby, set to run some number of times. And 

SECONDS = 0.1

def test_mult_of_3_not_5_is_fizz
  end_time = Time.now + SECONDS
  while Time.now < end_time do
    number = generate_mult_of_3_not_5()
    expected = 'Fizz'
    actual = fizzbuzz(number)
    assert(actual == expected)
  end
end
here it is set to run for some number of seconds, in this case a fraction of a second.  And you could certainly use both constraints at once.

However, the real magic of a property-based testing framework is in something much more difficult, called “shrinking”.  Not quite all tools do this, but the idea is to find the edges, by searching between an input that passes the test, and one that doesn’t, when it finds one of each.

Fizzbuzz has a repeating cycle of 15, but in many other cases, the rules or results change across the domain.  Let’s look at 

@n: Integer
def stupid_function(Integer n)
  1 / ((n / 5.0).round - 10)
end
a simple function that divides 1 by an input integer, but first it takes that input, divides it by five, rounds to the nearest integer, and subtracts ten.  Our output should be a floating point number in the range -1 to 1, but in some range, assuming we haven’t taken steps to avoid it, we’re going to have a division by zero error.  That would be the range where dividing by five and rounding gets us ten, or in other words, without rounding we get 9.6 to 10.4, or in other other words, 
47 -> -1
48 -> /0 ERROR
49 -> /0 ERROR
50 -> /0 ERROR
51 -> /0 ERROR
52 -> /0 ERROR
53 ->  1
48 to 52. 

Suppose we've told our tool the input range is integers from 1 to 100, and it successfully does 1, and 100, and then tries 50, but of course blows up.  First of course it tells us that, and now we know our truly valid input range isn't quite what we thought it was. 

But wait!  There's more!

It figures, if I may anthropomorphize it a bit, that there is probably an edge case, possibly multiple, somewhere between 50 and each of those numbers.  Let's look at it trying to find the edge case between 1 and 100.  It will basically do a binary search, trying halfway at 
26 -> -1
38 -> -1
44 -> -1
47 -> -1
49 -> /0 ERROR
48 -> /0 ERROR
26, the middle of the remainder (so basically 3/4 of the way) at 38, 7/8 of the way at 44, 15/16 of the way at 47, and finally gets another error, 31/32 of the way up, at 49.  There's only one number left to investigate, 48, which gives it another error, so now it can report to us that it found an edge case between 47 and 48.  Most of the tools will also investigate the other edge, using a similar technique, and tell you about the edge between 52 and 53.

So is this a
silver bullet? Of course not -

Fred Brooks,
author of
"No Silver Bullet —
Essence and Accident in
Software Engineering"
(1986 paper)
Fred Brooks told us back in 1986 that there’s no such thing.  It’s great for problems where valid inputs can be fairly easily defined in such a way that they can be created randomly, and the relationship to the output is easy to check, but it’s not so great for things with heavy side effects or lots of stored state.  However, even when dealing with such complex problems, at least some parts can usually be broken down into more suitable ones, with some effort.
